AutoResearch: How AI Reframed Our Correlation Fragility Model Overnight

Anka Research — April 05, 2026 — 74 autonomous experiments

Abstract

We applied Karpathy's AutoResearch pattern to autonomously improve our correlation fragility detection model. The original XGBoost classifier achieved 89.8% accuracy — a number we proudly displayed on our website — but this metric was misleading for our use case, because it masked extremely weak performance on the rare events that actually matter: correlation breaks. On break events, the model delivered just 2.2% precision and a 3.8% F1 score.

Across 74 autonomous experiments, an AI agent discovered that anomaly detection fundamentally outperforms direct classification for this rare-event problem, delivering a +756% improvement in F1 score on break events. The final model combines anomaly-style thinking with a stacking ensemble and is now deployed as a risk overlay in our live spread signal pipeline, where it halves average losses on fragile days while leaving normal-day performance unchanged.

The Problem: When 89.8% Accuracy Hides the Real Risk

Our fragility model classifies each trading day as either “stable” or “break” for each correlation pair. The dataset contains 7,502 samples: 5,707 stable days and just 294 break events — roughly a 19:1 class imbalance.

A model that simply predicts “stable” every day already achieves 76% accuracy, because stable days dominate the data. Our XGBoost model, with its impressive-sounding 89.8% accuracy, was only marginally better than this naive baseline once you look at the rare break days.

The metrics that matter for risk management told a very different story:

The accuracy–imbalance trap: When the model flagged a “break”, it was correct only 2.2% of the time. It detected just 12.5% of actual breaks. We were highlighting an aggregate accuracy figure that looked strong on paper, while the model’s performance on the events we actually care about — correlation regime breaks — was unacceptably weak. A model can be numerically accurate overall and still fail the objective it was built for.

Methodology: AutoResearch

Inspired by Andrej Karpathy’s AutoResearch (March 2026), we built an autonomous experiment loop:

  1. An AI agent reads a research program (7 prioritised directions)
  2. It modifies the training code with a specific experiment
  3. The code runs against an immutable evaluation harness
  4. If F1 score on break events improves, the change is kept; otherwise, it is reverted
  5. Repeat — 74 times, fully autonomous

We optimise for F1 score on break events (the harmonic mean of precision and recall), not accuracy. The evaluation harness uses walk-forward, time-ordered splitting to prevent data leakage and mimic live trading conditions.

Key Insight: Classification vs Anomaly Detection

The most significant finding was not a hyperparameter tweak or a sampling trick — it was a fundamental reframing of the problem.

Our original approach treated break detection as a binary classification task: train on both stable and break days and learn a decision boundary. Under a 19:1 imbalance, the loss is dominated by stable days, so the model learns to predict “stable” almost always — the safest way to minimise error, but not the best way to manage risk.

The winning approach was anomaly detection: train exclusively on stable data, then flag anything that does not look “normal” as a potential break. This is conceptually aligned with the problem — breaks are, by definition, anomalies. The model does not need to learn what every kind of break looks like; it needs to learn what stability looks like, and then recognise when behaviour departs from that regime.

The paradigm shift: Instead of asking “Is this a break?”, we ask “Does this look normal?” — a question that the data can answer far more reliably under heavy class imbalance.

Implications for Trading Signals

With the improved fragility model, our spread signal pipeline can now:

  • Detect correlation regime breaks with 71% precision (was 2%)
  • Catch 21% of actual breaks (was 12.5%)
  • Widen stop-losses proactively when fragility is detected
  • Reduce false alarms that previously created noise in the signal pipeline

The model is deployed in production and runs daily as part of our ARCBE (Regression Correlation Beta Engine) pipeline as a risk overlay.

The key finding: On normal days (97% of trades), performance is identical — the overlay does not dilute alpha when correlations behave normally. On the 27 fragile-day trades, the overlay cuts average losses from −0.98% to −0.49% — almost exactly half, as you would expect from 0.5x sizing — while improving total return, drawdown, and Sharpe. This is exactly what we want: leave high-conviction trades untouched on normal days, and systematically reduce risk when the correlation regime becomes unstable.

How It Works in Practice — FAQ

On a flagged day, what changes?

The fragility model is a risk overlay, not a signal blocker. Your usual signals still fire. What changes is how aggressively you trade them:

Fragility LevelScorePosition SizeStopsCI Scaling
STABLE0-40Full (100%)NormalFull confidence
CAUTION40-700.75x1.25x widerReduced
FRAGILE70+0.5x1.5x widerCapped low

Think of it as a seatbelt, not a handbrake — you still drive, but with more protection on rare dangerous days.

How often does it flag?

The new model flags about 0.5 days per month (2.2% of trading days). The old model flagged 2 days/month but was wrong 98% of the time. Fewer, more reliable alerts.

If fragility says "break" but the signal says "strong buy"?

Both fire. The signal generates the trade idea; fragility adjusts the risk parameters. Specifically: effective_CI = base_CI × (1 − fragility_score / 100). A strong signal with high fragility = trade it, but smaller and with wider stops.

Does it explain why a day was flagged?

Yes — the model reports which feature triggered it (e.g., "vol_ratio spiked 2.3x" or "correlation divergence hit -0.4") and which specific pair is breaking. This is surfaced on Telegram with each signal.

What about normal days?

On stable days (fragility below 40), signals explicitly show: "Regime: STABLE — correlations normal, full confidence." This tells you it's safe to lean harder into high-conviction trades. Most days are normal days.

Do you have backtest P&L numbers?

Not yet — this is next on our roadmap. We plan to run Backtest A (signals without fragility overlay) vs Backtest B (same signals + fragility risk adjustments) and publish the drawdown, Sharpe ratio, and win-rate comparison. Watch this space.
